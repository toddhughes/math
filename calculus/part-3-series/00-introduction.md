# Part 3 -- Series

This part is a different mode of calculus thinking. Up to now, our
question has always been "what is this function doing locally?" or
"what total does it accumulate?" Now we ask a stranger question:
"can we *rebuild* the function out of an infinite sum of simpler
pieces?"

It sounds wild that you can add up infinitely many numbers and get
something finite, but sometimes you can. Understanding *when* and
*how* unlocks a powerful idea: most of the functions you have ever
met -- $\sin x$, $\cos x$, $e^x$, $\ln(1 + x)$, and many others --
can be rewritten as infinite polynomials. That representation is the
**Taylor series**, and it is one of the most useful tools in all of
mathematics. It is what your calculator uses to compute $\sin(1.7)$.

The arc of Part 3:

1. We start with **sequences**, lists of numbers that go on forever,
   and ask whether they settle on a final value.
2. From sequences we move to **series** -- the sum of a sequence --
   and learn what it means for an infinite sum to have a finite
   total.
3. We meet the various **convergence tests** that let us decide
   whether a given series adds up to something finite, without
   having to compute the sum directly. There are several of these
   because different series surrender to different tests.
4. We then learn about **power series**, series whose terms involve
   a variable. These are infinite polynomials, and they behave in a
   shockingly orderly way.
5. The climax is the **Taylor series**. It tells you that if you
   know all the derivatives of a function at a single point, you
   know the function everywhere. This is the result that ties Part
   3 back to Part 1 in the most satisfying way: the local view
   (derivatives) reconstructs the global view (the function itself).
6. The part closes with **parametric equations** and **polar
   coordinates**, two alternative ways of describing curves that we
   will need in Part 4.

By the end of Part 3 you will see calculus as something larger than
a calculator for rates and totals. You will have seen its capacity
for *approximation*, the ability to replace something complicated
($\sin x$) with something simple (a polynomial) that is as accurate
as you need. That capacity is one of the reasons calculus matters in
practice.
