# Part 1 -- Derivatives

This part is about **instantaneous change**: how to measure how fast
something is happening *right now*, even when the thing in question
is moving, growing, or changing in some irregular way.

The arc of Part 1:

1. We start with the **limit** -- the technical tool that lets us
   talk rigorously about "getting infinitely close to" some value.
   Limits are the foundation that everything else in calculus is
   built on, so we spend several chapters getting comfortable with
   them and with the related idea of **continuity**.
2. With limits in hand we define the **derivative**, the precise
   number that captures the rate of change at a single instant.
3. Going back to the limit for every derivative would be exhausting.
   So next we build up a kit of **differentiation rules** that let
   us compute derivatives quickly: power, product, quotient, chain,
   and the derivatives of the standard functions (exponentials,
   logarithms, and the trig functions).
4. Finally we apply derivatives to the kinds of real-world problems
   they were invented for: **optimization** (finding the largest or
   smallest value of something), **related rates** (how the rate of
   one quantity controls the rate of another), **curve sketching**
   (using derivatives to draw a function's graph accurately), and
   **linear approximation** (using a derivative to estimate a
   function's value nearby). We close with **L'Hôpital's rule**,
   which uses derivatives to rescue limits that ordinary algebra
   cannot resolve.

By the end of Part 1, you will be fluent in the local view of a
function: at any point, you will be able to say how fast it is
changing, in which direction it is curving, and what value it is
about to take next. That fluency is what Part 2 will turn into the
ability to talk about totals built up over an entire interval.
