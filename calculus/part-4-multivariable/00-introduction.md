# Part 4 -- Multivariable Calculus

This part takes everything from Parts 1, 2, and 3 and lifts it into
higher dimensions. Until now, every function has had a single input
and a single output. From here on, functions can take in vectors and
may return vectors. The whole machine still works, but it grows new
moving parts.

A few new things become possible:

- We can describe **surfaces** in three-dimensional space, not just
  curves.
- We can ask not just "how does $f$ change as $x$ changes" but
  "how does $f$ change as we move in *this* direction at *that*
  speed?" That is what the **gradient** is for.
- We can integrate over regions of the plane, regions of space,
  along curves, and across surfaces.
- We can talk about **vector fields**: arrows attached to every
  point of space, like the velocity of water in a river or the pull
  of gravity in a region.

The arc of Part 4:

1. We start with **vectors and the geometry of space** -- the
   language we will use throughout this part.
2. **Vector-valued functions** describe curves in space, and they
   are how we will calculate things like the length of a piece of a
   curve or the speed of a particle moving along it.
3. **Functions of several variables** open the door to **partial
   derivatives**, the multivariable version of "rate of change," and
   to the **gradient**, the vector that points in the direction of
   steepest increase.
4. We learn to **optimize** functions of several variables,
   including the elegant **Lagrange multiplier** technique for
   optimizing under constraints.
5. **Double and triple integrals** let us integrate over regions of
   the plane and of space. Changing coordinates -- to polar,
   cylindrical, or spherical -- often makes such integrals
   dramatically easier.
6. The part culminates in the major theorems of **vector calculus**:
   the Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals, **Green's theorem**,
   the **divergence theorem**, and **Stokes' theorem**. These all
   look like different results, but they are not. Each of them is a
   higher-dimensional version of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus
   from Part 2, dressed in different geometry. By the end of Part 4
   you will see that the climax of single-variable calculus was, in
   retrospect, only one face of a much larger and more beautiful
   pattern.

That recognition -- that all the major theorems of calculus are the
same theorem in different costumes -- is the closing image of the
book.
