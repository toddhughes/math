# Part 2 -- Integrals

This part is about **accumulation**: how to add up infinitely many
infinitely small contributions to get a total. Just as the derivative
of Part 1 answered "what is the rate right now?", an integral
answers "what is the total so far?"

The arc of Part 2:

1. We start with the **antiderivative**. If we know how a quantity
   is changing, can we recover the quantity itself? This is exactly
   the reverse of what we did in Part 1, and it is the easy entry
   point to integration.
2. Next we define the **definite integral** -- not as a reverse
   derivative, but as a limit of sums, the way Archimedes might have
   measured a curved area if he had had the tool. This is the
   genuine geometric meaning of an integral.
3. Then comes the **Fundamental Theorem of Calculus**, one of the
   most important results in all of mathematics. The two halves of
   the book -- the local view of Part 1 and the global view of Part
   2 -- look like they are about completely different things.
   They are not. The Fundamental Theorem says that differentiation
   and integration are essentially inverse operations. This single
   result is the punchline of single-variable calculus, and it gets
   the prominence it deserves here.
4. With the foundations in place we learn the **techniques** that
   let us compute integrals that do not surrender to a single trick:
   substitution, integration by parts, trigonometric methods, and
   partial fractions. We also handle **improper integrals**, where
   the interval or the function itself is infinite.
5. We then use integrals to compute what they were invented for:
   **areas** between curves, **volumes** of solids of revolution,
   **arc length**, **surface area**, **work**, and the **average
   value** of a varying quantity.
6. The part closes with an introduction to **differential
   equations**, equations describing how a quantity changes with
   respect to some other quantity. They are the natural language of
   physics, biology, economics, and engineering, and the simplest
   ones can be solved using nothing more than the integrals we have
   just learned.

By the end of Part 2 you will have a complete view of single-variable
calculus: how to talk about a function locally (derivatives), how to
talk about it globally (integrals), and how the two languages fit
together (the Fundamental Theorem). Part 3 will then use these tools
in a new mode -- approximating functions by infinite sums.
