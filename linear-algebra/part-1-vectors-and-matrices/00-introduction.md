# Part 1 -- Vectors, Matrices, and Linear Systems

This part is the concrete computational foundation of linear algebra.
We start with vectors and their geometry, then turn to systems of
linear equations and the matrix algebra needed to solve them.

The arc of Part 1:

1. We begin with **vectors** -- lists of numbers with a rich
   geometric interpretation. We learn how to add and scale them, how
   to measure their lengths, and how to compute the angle between
   two vectors using the **dot product**.
2. We use vector arithmetic to describe **lines and planes** in
   space, the simplest geometric objects after points.
3. We turn to **systems of linear equations**, the original
   motivating problem for linear algebra. We introduce **matrices**
   as compact notation for systems, and **Gaussian elimination**
   (also called row reduction) as the systematic algorithm for
   solving any system, no matter how large.
4. We develop **matrix arithmetic** in its own right: addition,
   scalar multiplication, and the surprisingly subtle operation of
   **matrix multiplication**.
5. We meet the **inverse** of a matrix, which lets us "undo" a
   matrix multiplication and gives us a direct algebraic method for
   solving systems when an inverse exists.
6. We close with **determinants**, a single number computed from a
   square matrix that tells us at a glance whether the matrix is
   invertible, and that has a beautiful geometric interpretation as
   a signed volume.

By the end of Part 1 you will be able to solve any system of linear
equations, perform matrix arithmetic confidently, decide whether a
matrix is invertible, and reason geometrically about lines, planes,
areas, and volumes. Part 2 will then take these concrete tools into
their abstract setting -- vector spaces and linear transformations.
